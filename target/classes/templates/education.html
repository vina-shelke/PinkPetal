Uterus Anatomy & Structure

The female reproductive system is complex, beautiful, and highly synchronized. Here is a breakdown of the key anatomical structures:

  • Ovaries: Two small glands that house and release eggs (ovum) and produce essential sex hormones (estrogen and progesterone).
  • Fallopian Tubes: Narrow ducts connecting ovaries to the uterus, where egg fertilization normally occurs.
  • Uterus (Womb): A pear-shaped, muscular organ where a fetus grows. Its inner lining, the endometrium, thickens and sheds during each cycle.
  • Cervix: The lower, narrow canal connecting the uterus body with the vagina.

Hormonal Cycles & Phases

The menstrual cycle is driven by cyclical fluctuations in four key hormones: Estrogen, Progesterone, LH, and FSH. The cycle has 4 distinct phases:

Phase Name Estimated Days Hormone Profile What Happens in Your Body
Menstrual Phase Days 1 - 5 Low Estrogen & Progesterone The thickened endometrium lining sheds, causing menstrual bleeding.
Follicular Phase Days 6 - 13 Rising Estrogen Estrogen thickens the lining again. Ovarian follicles mature an egg.
Ovulatory Phase Day 14 LH Surge, Peak Estrogen The ovary releases the mature egg into the fallopian tube. Fertile peak.
Luteal Phase Days 15 - 28 Peak Progesterone Progesterone maintains lining for potential pregnancy. Drops if no fertilization occurs.

Understanding PCOS

Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS) is a highly common hormonal condition that affects 1 in 10 women of reproductive age. It is characterized by imbalances in reproductive hormones.

Common Symptoms
  • Irregular or missing periods
  • High levels of androgen hormones (excess facial/body hair, severe acne)
  • Enlarged ovaries containing fluid-filled sacs (follicles)
  • Weight gain and difficulty losing weight
Management & Care
  • Balanced diet targeting insulin sensitivity
  • Regular moderate exercises
  • Medications (metformin or contraceptives prescribed by gynecologist)
  • Support and stress management

Understanding Endometriosis

Endometriosis is a condition where tissue similar to the lining of the uterus (endometrium) grows outside the womb. This tissue behaves like endometrium—it thickens, breaks down, and bleeds with each menstrual cycle, but has no way to exit the body, causing pain and inflammation.

Key Warning Signs
  • Extremely painful cramps (dysmenorrhea)
  • Chronic pelvic pain throughout the month
  • Pain during or after sexual intercourse
  • Excessive bleeding or spot bleeding between cycles
When to see a Doctor

If your menstrual cycle pain is so severe that it prevents you from doing daily activities, or if pain does not respond to common heating pad and OTC remedies, seek professional consultation. Early diagnosis helps manage pelvic scar tissue.
